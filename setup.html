Type of Headphones

Open vs Closed Headphones:

Open headphones are good for:

  • Surround sound
  • Home listening
  • Critical listening
  • Enjoying high-quality content
  • Longer wear-time

Open headphones are bad for:

  • Blocking outside noise
  • Listening on your commute or whilst traveling
  • Using as monitors when recording with microphones
  • listening on crowded areas

Closed headphones are good for:

  • On LAN, ex. Dreamhack or E-sport tournament
  • Casual listening
  • On travel
  • Recording audio
  • Communicating, ex. Discord
  • Blocking outside noise

Closed headphones are bad for:

  • Allowing heat to vent around your ears
  • Shorter wear-time
  • Accurate surround sound

What do we recommend?

We recommend Open headphones when It comes to gaming, longer wear-time and delivers wider soundstage aka surround sound. However If you are playing on LAN events or at crowded areas, we strongly recommend closed headphones In that scenarios

Equipment

Onboard audio:

Onboard audio delivers the sound you hear directly from your PC, this Is the most common setup. Today most motherboards onboard audio do the job for many headphones. For most users & gamers onboard audio will serve you well. If you don't get the volume you wan't even when It's turned 100%, you need an amplifier.

Downsides for Onboard audio:

  • No analog volume control
  • Not giving enough volume for power-hungry headphones

External audio:

External audio takes the digital signal from your PC and converts It to analog externally, also called DAC. Keep in mind that DAC and amplifier are 2 different things, both are required so we human can hear the sound. But most External audio devices has both DAC and amplifier included in one device, usually called: DAC & Amp combo. In short term, audio Is handled outside from PC. For gaming however, external audio can give some benefit in some scenarios, ex. manual volume control and getting enough power depending on what headphones you use. Buying a External DAC & Amp Is expensive, around 200$ and up. It can be waste of money for some users. We only recommend buying external audio If you love audio and want High-End equipment.

Software

Why Software?

Do you need software to get better audio performance? Answer Is no.

Often some audio software that comes included can degrade your listening experience. Cheap headphones might benefit using software but for high-end headphones It should be turned off. Only exception Is EQ setting that can benefit in some scenarios.

Some softwares tend to have some audio-feature effects turned on by default. Which can effect sound quality a lot for your headphones.

Cables

Does Cables matter?

Better quality cable can offer a range of benefits, but improved audio quality is not one of them. More rugged cable and connectors can be useful if you are regularly plugging and unplugging.

Analog cable myths:

Now, it is true that back in the day, cheaper cables were made with less-than-ideal components and had fairly poor quality control. When Monster cables hit the scene, people loved to bash how much they cost, but you did get a somewhat decent build quality to them for the investment, along with a lifetime warranty. But that was a long time ago, and nowadays you can get cables for dirt cheap that not only perform their function almost perfectly, but will also stand up to the test of time.

Digital cable myths:

What about digital? The previous logic doesn't hold up at all. These damn things carry ones and zeros: while you might need a powered USB hub to get a signal over long distances, these cables either work or they don't. Premium USB or optical cables will neverimprove sound quality.
